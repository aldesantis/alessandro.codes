## Summary

Zoe Scaman explains how crafting a great keynote is about deep thinking, storytelling, and presence on stage. She shows that speaking is a performance that connects ideas to people, not just sharing information. Every talk is practice that sharpens both your message and your ability to engage an audience.

## Highlights

- I don’t use notes. No script, no bullet points, no teleprompter. Nothing written down. I walk on stage with the whole thing in my head and nothing in my hands.
- Some people find the range confusing. “What does she actually do?” But the range is the point. The cross-pollination is what keeps the thinking sharp. Ideas from one territory illuminate another. The muscle that lets me talk about fandom is the same muscle that lets me talk about AI - because underneath, they’re both about how people find meaning, form identity, allocate attention.
- The opening matters more than anything else. Those first thirty seconds determine whether you have the room or you’ve already lost it. You don’t start with throat-clearing. You don’t start with “Today I’m going to talk about…” You don’t start with your credentials or a summary of what’s coming. All of that is a cue to zone out.
- Transitions are invisible work. The audience never consciously notices them, but they feel the difference between a talk that flows and one that lurches from topic to topic. You never just move to the next thing. You bridge. “That’s the trust piece. But trust doesn’t operate in isolation…” This is what makes it feel like one continuous thought instead of a series of disconnected points.
- Endings are not summaries. I cannot stress this enough. The instinct when you’re wrapping up is to recap what you just said - “So, to summarise, we talked about X, Y and Z…” That’s the energy of someone who doesn’t know how to land the plane. It deflates everything you’ve built.
  The last five minutes should feel like acceleration, not deceleration. A provocation that lingers. A question without an easy answer. A challenge that follows them out of the room and into the bar afterwards. Land it and leave. Don’t dilute the ending by restating what they’ve just heard.
- And everything - everything - is grounded. Real companies, specific numbers, named examples. Not abstract theorising but “here’s what’s actually happening.” If I’m making a point about AI changing creative work, I talk about what Klarna did, what’s happening inside Walmart, what the latest earnings calls reveal. Anchor every idea in something real.
- When slides look crafted - when there’s clearly been care and intention in every frame - the audience assumes the thinking has had the same care. They trust you more. They lean in more. The design signals: this person gives a shit. This person has sweated the details. This person respects my time enough to make this good.
- I design everything myself. I’ve never worked with a slide designer - partly because I’m a control freak, partly because the design process is where I figure out what I actually want to say. The act of making the visual forces me to sharpen the idea. If I can’t make it into a clear slide, maybe the idea isn’t clear yet.
- The point is: you don’t need to be a natural extrovert to do this. You don’t need to be the life of the party. You just need to find the ritual that gets you from wherever you are to ready.
- You can do more than you think. You can recover from more than you imagine. The fear never goes away, but the evidence accumulates: you’ve survived every talk so far. You’ll probably survive the next one too.
- Every stage is a chance to get better. It’s also a chance to fail. Usually it’s both at the same time.
