## Summary

Luxury no longer wins by scarcity because access and visibility have made goods common. Top brands regain power by shaping culture, not chasing growth or trends. They protect value through craft, curated experiences, and exclusive communities.

## Highlights

- Luxury has always derived its power from culture, not just commerce.
  Its cultural power—the symbolic authority, desirability, and social capital—justified high prices. Iconic products like Birkin, Rolex Daytona, or Chanel tweed, signal taste, identity, and belonging as much as one’s financial might.
- Commodification of luxury dilutes its cultural power, and in return, desirability of its products. Products are not signals when everyone has them; they are also not signals when they do not signal anything.
- Luxury’s winners carefully orchestrate product portfolios that balance icons, classics, limited editions, and collectibles to preserve exclusivity while appealing to varied consumer segments. Creativity and originality are strategic advantage over marketing spectacle. For example, Birkin bag’s enduring desirability creates a gravitational pull where every Hermès product, from a lipstick to scarf to a bag charm, feels like a small step toward that ultimate object.
