## Summary

Google will launch AI smart glasses this fall, partnering with Gentle Monster to combine technology and stylish design. These glasses use AI to help with tasks like searching, translating, and shopping, creating new opportunities for brands to connect with consumers. However, privacy concerns and consumer trust remain big challenges for the widespread use of this new technology.

## Highlights

- As it stands, frontier AI models like ChatGPT, Google Gemini, and Meta AI are each trained on bounded data sets — largely static snapshots of internet data like web crawls, licensed texts, and chatbot conversations. Smart glasses, however, can potentially capture what you look at, dwell upon, where you go, what you buy, who you speak to, your routines, environmental data, and emotional signals, along with all the other sensory aspects of your day. That gives the tech companies behind these glasses a privileged position over search, shopping, navigation, memory, payments, messaging, entertainment, and personal assistance. It’s a contextual data goldmine as useful to the tech companies racing to build humankind’s primary AI interface as it is to fashion brands wanting to advertise and intercept purchase intent.
- “Advertising will become much more intimate as it moves closer to the moment of intent,” says WGSN’s Aquino, pointing to how all this context paves the way for AI assistants to intervene exactly at the point when desire is still forming, even subconsciously. “If someone looks twice at a bag, pauses outside a restaurant, asks whether a jacket suits them, or compares two products in a store, that is commercial data,” she adds. “It can recommend, compare, discount, nudge, and complete the purchase in one flow.”
- Fashion softens the data mining and surveillance layers, making devices feel socially wearable — even before society has worked out the rules around bystander privacy, data extraction, and AI mediation.
- “Silicon Valley’s current obsession with taste and storytelling matters: it’s trying to make technological power feel aspirational, human, and culturally literate.”
