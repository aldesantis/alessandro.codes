## Summary

Strategy is about making sense of complex problems by connecting ideas from many different fields. Zoe Scaman shows that broad thinking, not deep expertise in one area, helps people see new solutions. This wide approach is valuable beyond traditional brand or campaign work.

## Highlights

- Every single time, the same inner critic running its mouth: this is terrifying, I’m not qualified, nobody asked me to be the expert, you’re a fraud, someone’s going to rumble you and it’s going to be mortifying. That voice is loud. It always goes first. But there’s another one behind it, quieter, slower to show up: well no one else is connecting these dots, no one else is standing where I’m standing. So why not me?
- That feeling - the terror followed by the moment where a connection lands that nobody expected, least of all you - is the most addictive thing I’ve ever experienced professionally. More than any award. More than any big agency pitch win. The pure thrill of being inside a problem you have no business being inside, and finding that your brain works there too.
- Somewhere along the way we let “strategy” shrink to mean brands and campaigns, when really it was about the range of your inputs, the quality of your thinking, and where you chose to point it.
- I’m not walking in pretending to know more about pharmaceuticals than people who’ve spent their entire careers in that industry. I’m there because the people who’ve spent their careers inside a system can’t always see past it. They know the landscape intimately - but intimacy breeds blind spots. I’m there to scour the landscape they already know and then bring entirely new ones into the spotlight. From abstract categories. From completely different economic models. From fields of specialisation that have never crossed their desk and wouldn’t, unless someone dragged them in from outside.
