## Summary

Chaos theory studies how simple systems can produce complex, unpredictable behavior. Tiny changes in initial conditions can lead to very different outcomes. It appears in physics, weather, biology, and many other fields.

## Highlights

- Small differences in initial conditions, such as those due to errors in measurements or due to rounding errors in [numerical computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_analysis), can yield widely diverging outcomes for such dynamic systems, rendering long-term prediction of their behavior impossible in general.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory/#cite_note-7) This can happen even though these systems are [deterministic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deterministic_system_(mathematics)), meaning that their future behavior follows a unique evolution[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory/#cite_note-:2-8) and is fully determined by their initial conditions, with no [random](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomness) elements involved.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory/#cite_note-9) In other words, despite the deterministic nature of these systems, this does not make them predictable.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory/#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory/#cite_note-WerndlCharlotte-11)
- Chaos: When the present determines the future but the approximate present does not approximately determine the future.
- Chaos theory concerns deterministic systems which are predictable for some amount of time and then appear to become random.
- The amount of time for which the behavior of a chaotic system can be effectively predicted depends on three things: How much uncertainty can be tolerated in the forecast, how accurately its current state can be measured, and a time scale depending on the dynamics of the system, called the [Lyapunov time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyapunov_time).
